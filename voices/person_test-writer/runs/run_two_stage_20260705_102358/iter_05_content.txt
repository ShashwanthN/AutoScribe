### The Lab Tool That Nobody Wanted

Yeah, I thought we had it figured out.
We built an AI analysis tool for labs.
We had good people, plenty of cash, a clean interface.
I sent it to a bunch of labs for free.
I checked back weeks later and none of them were using it.
I kept hearing they just needed report writers instead.
Not a new tool.

I spent a ton of cash and months on that.
You can build the best thing in the room and it means nothing if the room is empty.

### What I learned about Building

I used to think building hard could make anything work - the empty room proved that wrong.
I saw rough projects win when they found the right crowd.
We were just offering something nobody asked for.

### The Conference Pivot

I fixed it after a random chat at a conference.
I met a guy from a coatings company.
He had a completely different headache.
I used the same engine for a different crowd.
I changed the message.
I changed the audience.

I wasted so much time and cash on that detour. Costly af.

### How I check for the real problem now

I used to just build something and hope.
I look at the space first.

I start by looking at what is trending.
I scrape forums to see what people are complaining about.
I run it through GPT to find the pattern.
It felt a bit like being in a heist movie , finding the clue before the big job.

Then I test the way I talk about it.
I tested a few ways of saying it. I found one way worked way better.
I checked who was actually talking about it online.

Then i put a tiny amount of cash into ads.
I got a bunch of people who said they wanted it.
Only then did I build the real thing.

I find a beta shows you how people use your specific fix.
I find scraping shows you the problem as it lives in the wild.
I prefer seeing the problem first.

### What do I look for in the noise? (in my POV)

Once I have the raw data, I look for a few things.
I look at how often the headache shows up or what language they use or what they are paying for alternatives.
I look for the difference between a loud few people and a real crowd.

When the structure of the problem gets messy, I map it out visually.
I use a drawing tool to map the fix to the crowd.
I find it helps me see the fit before I build.

### Bending the idea

I used the same tech from my lab project. I just pointed it at a different crowd.
It is way easier to build for a specific pinch than a vague ache.
I saw the floor project work the same way.
I didn't build a general floor thing.
I built exactly for the crowd i found.

### When I decide to walk

It is the hardest part. Walking away.

I ask myself if I could sell this to a random person.
Just based on their own headache.
Without my story or my team.
I ask myself. Would they buy it in a few sentences?

If the answer is no, it is a fix for a problem nobody has.
I find a weekend of checking is way cheaper than a few months of building.
I find the cheapest fix is the one you make before you write a line of code.

### The real trick

I try to resist the urge to build first.
I write down who needs it and what they struggle with.
I scrape the space to see if people are talking about it.
I test how I describe it.
I check if that audience is real.

I find the person who checks first wins.
It comes down to what people actually want.

TL;DR I check the problem before I build the fix.
