**Market First (in my POV)**

**Who Actually Wants What You Built? (in my POV)**

Imagine spending six months of runway and two of the best AI engineers i know on a platform that hits 97.1% accuracy and cuts analysis time by 95%.

Sounds like a slam dunk, right?

Until you send 50 free trials to top-tier research labs and get zero active users back.

Follow-up calls reveal the ugly truth. They don't have a tool problem. They have a people problem. "We just need report writers, not a new tool."

Yeah, that stings - it's the market giving you a death sentence dressed up as polite feedback.

The founder thought speed and capital were enough. The market disagreed. The gap between believing you are right and proving it isn't a matter of grit - it's a matter of validation.

**Why Execution is a Trap (in my POV)**

A lot of founders fall for the same trap - the idea that sheer execution velocity can bend reality. Build fast, raise more, code through the night, and the market will follow.

This comes from VC folklore. Survivorship bias. We hear about the founders who coded through YC and won. We don't hear about the ones who coded a perfect product into an empty room.

Market fit is primary. Execution is just table stakes.

You can't code a market into existence.

Execution exploits or amplifies an *existing* external need. It rarely invents a market from scratch. Fracto's eloquent zero is the proof. Perfect product, wrong conversation.

The question is never whether to execute. It is whether to execute in a market that wants what you're building.

**What Changed? (in my POV)**

So what saved Fracto?

A chance conversation at a random conference. A quality engineer from a specialty coatings manufacturer starts complaining about inconsistent failure analysis scrapping millions of dollars.

The founder has an "oh" moment.

The same AI engine. Different market. Different message.

Before: Research Labs. Zero traction. Product rejected.
After: Manufacturing QA. Strong traction. Product valued.

The technology did not change. The *market definition* changed. The *message* changed. The product shaped itself around the market's actual pain.

That detour took six months and nearly burned the company. Here is the rescue kit to skip that step entirely.

**How Do You Validate Before You Build? (in my POV)**

The most expensive mistake is spending $50k+ on a polished MVP before proving the problem exists at scale.

Common fixes for the Fracto situation?

Why does "Ship faster" fail?
Because it accelerates direction, doesn't validate destination.

Why does "More funding" fail?
Because it bets bigger on an untested thesis.

Why does "User beta" fail?
Because it biases towards your specific solution to an assumption.

The principle is simple: Validate the problem organically *before* coding the solution.

Here is the pipeline i use:
- Trend Detection (Exploding Topics).
- Pain Scraping (Apify).
- Analysis (GPT).
- Message Confirmation (PickFu).
- Audience Confirmation (SparkToro).

How it played out for FloorOps:
- Step 1 Trend: Exploding Topics flagged "industrial epoxy floor complaints" rising.
- Step 2 Scrape: Apify scraped 500 Reddit threads (DIY, home improvement, garage).
- Step 3 Analyse: GPT analysis extracted a cluster: "Military-grade coatings for home garages."
- Step 4 Confirm: PickFu tested 3 messages. One over-indexed 3x.
- Step 5 Audience: SparkToro confirmed the audience (DIY car guys, home mechanics).

Result? $500 ad spend -> 800 waitlist signups. *Then* they built the MVP. The problem was validated Af.

Why this works now: AI tools make this pipeline cheap and fast (<1 week, <$1k). The cost of building is low, so the advantage belongs to the best validators, not the fastest builders.

I know what a funded founder is thinking right now. "I'd rather spend $50k and three months getting real usage data from a beta than sit around scraping Reddit."

Respectfully - an MVP beta gives you engagement metrics on *your specific solution* to an assumed problem. It does not validate the *problem* itself. Scraping validates the organic existence and severity of the pain. One prevents the other from being a waste of money.

Validate the problem, not just the solution.

**What Does a Real Signal Look Like? (in my POV)**

Once you have the raw signal, the real work begins: extracting the decision from the noise.

Frequency of Pain: How often does the same exact problem appear in scraped data? If it's a one-off, it's a feature request, not a market.
Intensity of Pain: What language does the user use? Desperation vs. optimisation vs. curiosity. "This is ruining my business" is better than "it would be nice to have".
Pricing Sensitivity: PickFu results showing what they are willing to pay versus free alternatives.
Signal vs. Noise: Distinguishing between a loud minority and a real market segment.

When the signal structure gets complex, mapping it visually helps. i use draw.io MCP to map the problem/solution/target structure. [Guide link here]. This is a structured way to see the fit before you build.

**When Do You Let the Market Shape the Idea? (in my POV)**

With a clear analysis of the signal, you have to let it change your plan.

The product does not invent the market. The market shapes the product.

The willingness to discard features, change buyer personas, or abandon entire use cases based on the analysis is what separates the winners.

Call back to Fracto: The tech didn't change. The *application* did (Research -> QA).
Call back to FloorOps: They didn't build a general "floor tool". They built specifically for the validated niche.

It is easier to build for a pinched nerve than a vague ache.

**When Do You Walk Away? (in my POV)**

But shaping only works if the signals point to a real, viable need. Sometimes the smartest move is to fold.

What metric or human sentence tells you to cut bait? Revisit the Fracto rejection signal. "We just need report writers." That's your trigger.

The hard question: If you had to sell this to a stranger for *their* pain, with no founder charisma or equity story, would they buy it in 30 seconds? Or is this a solution in search of a problem?

A weekend of validation is cheaper than six months of failure. A pivot is not a failure - failing to validate is.

The cheapest pivot is the one you make before you write a line of production code.
