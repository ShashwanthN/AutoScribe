### The Hook
i watched a team do everything right.
Good engineers. Clean code. Money in the bank.
It felt like that scene in a heist movie where they drill into the wrong vault.
All that prep. Wrong room.
They built a tool for labs that analyze fractures.
Really good accuracy. Made it way faster.
They sent a bunch of free trials to top labs.
A month later , zero people were using it.
i called them up.
"We just need people to write reports , not a new tool," the guy said.
Ouch.
They spent months on it. Morale tanked.
The assumption was the killer.

### The Indictment
Yeah i get it - everyone says just build fast and the market will follow.
i used to believe that too.
You hear stories about founders grinding through the night.
But i saw the lab tool fail. Perfect build. Nobody cared.
Then i saw another thing. A scrappier setup. Weaker code.
But they picked the right people who actually had the problem.
It took off.
Made me realize you can't code a want into existence.

### The Turning Point
For the first team , the fix came in a random chat at a conference.
A guy from a coatings company was annoyed about analysis costs eating his budget.
He didn't want a researcher tool.
He wanted consistent QA for their shop floor.
Same engine. Different people. Different message.
That detour cost half a year. Expensive Af.

### Why I check first (in my POV)
Because building the wrong thing is way more expensive than asking around first.
Because a weekend of digging saves months of fixing.
Because the tools are finally cheap enough to do this.

### How i read the room
There are tools that tell you what people are complaining about.
i used one to search for "industrial epoxy complaints". It was popping off.
i scraped a bunch of forum threads. Fed them into an AI.
It found a cluster: people wanting tough coatings for their home garages.
i tested a few messages with a poll. One did three times better.
i checked who the audience was. DIY guys.
i threw a few bucks at ads. Got a bunch of names for the waiting list.
Then we built the thing. It felt like cheating.

### What the process taught me
The product doesn't invent the need. The need shapes the product.
For the lab tool , the tech didn't change. The application did.
For the epoxy thing , we didn't build a general tool. We built exactly for that garage crowd.
It is easier to build for a specific itch than a general ache.

### When i decide to fold
i started asking a hard question.
If i had to sell this to a stranger with no hype , just their problem.
Would they buy it in 30 seconds?
If the answer is no , it's just a solution looking for a puzzle.
A weekend of checking is cheaper than six months of building wrong.
Folding early feels bad. It beats the alternative.

### My new rule
Now i do a quick lap before i build anything.
i write down who it's for , what their problem is.
i scrape the complaints. i poll the message. i confirm the crowd.
Takes a weekend. Saves months.
The person checking first has the real edge.

TL;DR
Building fast doesn't fix a bad guess. Checking first does.
