### The hook

i built this incredible fracture analysis tool for research labs. Poured months into it. Gave it out for free to a bunch of labs.

A month later , no one was using it.

i asked around. The gist was they just needed people to write up reports , not some new software.

i used to think speed and money could fix anything. i was wrong. The gap between being sure you're right and finding out you aren't isn't about grinding harder. It's about checking first.

### The big mistake

i see a lot of people , myself included once , who think a perfect build makes demand appear.

i see stories of overnight success. They make it sound like pure grit and coding through the night work.

My lab software was technically perfect. Completely ignored. Like being the best cookie baker in a town that only wants cake.

i've seen the opposite too. It was a person with a rougher product but a crowd that desperately wanted it.

From what i've seen , building just fills an existing need better. You can't code a crowd into existence.

### How it changed

i had a random chat at a conference that changed the whole thing. i met a guy from a coatings place. He had a completely different hassle. He needed consistent failure analysis. My software did exactly that.

Same code. Different crowd. Different way of explaining what it did.

The detour cost me months and a big chunk of my money.

### Why i check first? (in my POV)

- Because the most expensive thing i ever did was build a polished version before i knew the hassle was real.
- Because building faster just gets you to the wrong crowd quicker.
- Because more money just makes the wrong bet bigger.

I do a routine now.

- I look at trend tools to see if something is growing.
- I scrape forums to see what people complain about.
- I use a language model to group the complaints.
- I test a few messages to see which one sticks.
- I check if the crowd is real.

Once i saw epoxy floor complaints rising. i grabbed a bunch of Reddit threads. The model found a pattern around military-grade coatings for home garages. i tested a few messages. One did way better. i found the crowd was DIY car guys.

A small ad spend got me a few hundred names. i built the rough version afterwards.

A beta tests your specific guess. Scraping forums tests if the hassle is real before you make a guess. i prefer the second one.

### Reading the signs

i look at how often the same complaint shows up on reddit or twitter or a forum.

i check the language. Are the people desperate or just curious?

i see what they already pay for a patch.

It's easy to mistake a loud few for a real crowd.

When the pattern gets messy , i map it out with a diagram tool. Helps me see the shape of it.

### Bend the code to the crowd

i let the tech bend towards the crowd. Not what i feel like building.

i dropped whole features. Changed who i thought the buyers were.

Lab research was a no. Manufacturing QA was a yes. Same engine.

FloorOps wasn't a general floor tool. It was strictly for that garage crowd i found.

### Knowing when to fold

i think the hardest skill is knowing when to walk away.

What tells me to cut bait? Rejection. That experience was a big red flag.

i ask if a stranger would say yes in a few seconds about their own hassle. If not , it's a tough road.

A weekend of checking is way cheaper than months of building. Letting go of a bad idea isn't failure. Not checking first is.

### The last thing

The shift in my thinking was simple. i stopped rushing to build and started rushing to check if people had the thing i was fixing.

i agree it's exciting to start coding right away - a weekend of checking is a better use of the excitement.

My weekend routine:

- Write down who i think has the hassle.
- Scrape some forums to see if they talk about it.
- Test the message with a poll.
- Check if that crowd is real.

This is what i do now. It stops me wasting time on things people don't want.

The patient builder who checks first gets the advantage. i let the crowd decide.

The lab rejection was humbling Af.

TL;DR Check the hassle before you build the fix.
